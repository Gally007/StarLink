Red Dwarfs

A Red Dwarf is a small, faint star in the main sequence phase,
characterized by slow nuclear fusion processes and incredible longevity. These stars can
last trillions of years, outliving larger and more massive stars.

About Red Dwarfs

  • Small and Cool Stars: Red dwarfs are the smallest and coolest type of star, with a mass between 0.075 and 0.50 times that of the Sun.
  • Long Lifespan: Due to their slow fuel consumption, red dwarfs can live for billions to trillions of years, much longer than more massive stars.
  • Low Surface Temperature: They have a surface temperature between 2,500 and 4,000 K, giving them their characteristic red color.
  • Stable Hydrogen Fusion: Red dwarfs primarily burn hydrogen into helium, maintaining stability for most of their lifetimes.
  • Most Common Stars: Red dwarfs make up 70-80% of the stars in the Milky Way galaxy, though they are faint and difficult to see with the naked eye.
  • No Red Giant Phase: Unlike larger stars, red dwarfs do not expand into red giants but instead burn hydrogen for much longer periods.
