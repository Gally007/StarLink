Main Sequence Stars

Main sequence stars fuse hydrogen atoms to form helium atoms in their cores.
About 90 percent of the stars in the universe, including the sun, are main sequence stars.
These stars can range from about a tenth of the mass of the sun to up to 200 times as massive. Stars start their
lives as clouds of dust and gas. Gravity draws these clouds together. A small protostar forms, powered by the collapsing material. Protostars often form in densely packed clouds of gas and can be challenging to detect.

About Main Sequence Stars:

Main Sequence Stars generate energy by fusing hydrogen into helium, producing
light and heat. Their temperature and color depend on mass: hot, massive stars are blue or white
(25,000 K - 50,000 K), while cooler, smaller stars are orange or red (2,500 K - 6,000 K). Lifespans vary by size, with
massive stars living a few million years and smaller ones lasting billions or trillions of years. They come in sizes ranging from
smaller to larger than the Sun.
