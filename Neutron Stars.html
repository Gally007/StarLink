Neutron Stars

A neutron star is the dense, compact remnant of a massive star that has undergone a supernova explosion. When a star with a mass greater than about 8 times the Sun exhausts its nuclear fuel, its core collapses under gravity, compressing the matter to an extremely high density. This results in the formation of a neutron star.

About Neutron Stars

Neutron stars are exceptionally dense, with a sugar-cube-sized portion weighing billions of tons.
Despite their small size, typically 10-15 kilometers (6-9 miles) in diameter, they possess immense mass. Composed primarily of
neutrons, they are stabilized by neutron degeneracy pressure. These stars exhibit extremely strong magnetic fields and often rotate rapidly, sometimes
completing a rotation in milliseconds, emitting detectable beams of radiation as pulsars. Their compact structure results in extraordinarily strong gravitational forces.
