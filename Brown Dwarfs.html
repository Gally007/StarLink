Brown Dwarfs

A white dwarf is the final stage of a star like our Sun after it has exhausted its fuel. When a star runs out of energy, it sheds its outer layers, forming a glowing cloud called a planetary nebula, and leaves behind a small, dense core. This core, the white dwarf, is about the size of Earth but has a mass similar to the Sun, making it incredibly dense. Composed mainly of carbon and oxygen with a thin outer layer of hydrogen or helium, white dwarfs start very hot but cool and fade over billions of years, offering valuable insights into the life cycles of stars.

About Brown Dwarfs

  • Did you know that the Brown Dwarfs is a Celestial objects between planets and stars, often called "failed stars."
  • They are 13 to 80 times the mass of Jupiter, with surface temperatures ranging from 250 to 2,500 Kelvin, glowing faintly in infrared light.
  • They form like stars, from collapsing gas clouds, but lack the mass needed to ignite full nuclear fusion.
  • Brown dwarfs bridge the gap between planets and stars, helping scientists understand how these celestial bodies form and evolve.
